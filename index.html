This Is What It Looks Like In Practice

A founder recently asked a specific question: how do I sell more of these experience tours I love doing in NYC?

What I heard was something else entirely.

Three long-term clients she’s outgrown. A life she wants — part-time in France — that she’d half-convinced herself wasn’t realistic. And a quiet exit question underneath all of it that she hadn’t given herself permission to ask out loud.

She’d sold one tour to individual buyers. That’s not where her money is. The buyer isn’t the person on the tour — it’s the person who books tours for other people. DMCs. Event planners. Corporate experience officers.

My advice: keep pursuing this new love — but don’t focus on selling to individuals where the effort outweighs the payoff. Find the people who organize these experiences and give a tour away free to the decision-makers. They’ll be 75% sold before anyone signs anything. And sure — once she’s built it here, she can do the same thing in France. That’s not a pivot. That’s satisfaction. (And it’s also what makes this a repeatable, scalable business, should she ever want to turn it into something that sells.)

Sometimes the direction is immediately obvious — a V-8 moment where the founder can’t believe they didn’t see it sooner. Other times it’s more layered. Either way, it takes an outsider to find THE TELL — and from there, to start working on a real solution.
